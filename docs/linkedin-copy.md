## Post 2 — Seat math (Part3 angle, no competitor named)

> Here's the math nobody likes doing.
>
> Per-seat CA software at $150–200/user/month sounds fine — until you add the second PM, the spec writer who touches submittals twice a month, and the principal who just wants to see the pay app. Suddenly you're at $9,000+/year, and you're rationing logins.
>
> So firms do what rationing always causes: they route around the tool. CA ends up half in the platform, half in email. The record splits.
>
> We priced BuildCycle by active projects instead. From $39/month flat. Unlimited users. Every consultant, GC, and owner's rep collaborates free — because a CA record only works if everyone's actually in it.
>
> Priced by projects, not people. Join the waitlist → buildcycle.io

## Post 3 — "Why we'll never charge per seat" (philosophy / contrarian, discussion bait)

> An unpopular opinion about construction software pricing:
>
> Charging per seat is charging firms to keep people OUT of the record.
>
> Every per-seat platform creates the same quiet incentive. The principal who just wants to glance at the pay app? Not worth a license. The consultant who touches submittals twice a month? Skip them. The owner's rep? They can get it forwarded.
>
> So the people who should be in the CA record stay out of it — and the record stops being the record. It splits into the tool, plus six inboxes.
>
> BuildCycle will never do that. Flat pricing by active projects. Unlimited users, forever. Bring every consultant, every reviewer, the GC, the owner — collaboration is free, because a CA record only works when everyone's actually in it.
>
> That's not a launch promo. It's the model.
>
> Join the waitlist → buildcycle.io

## Post 4 — Field reports (ArchiSnapper angle)

> The site visit takes two hours. The field report takes three.
>
> Photos off the phone, into Word. Which wall was that? Cross-reference the plan sheet. Look up the weather. Format. Export. Send. Repeat next week.
>
> BuildCycle builds the report while you walk: observations marked directly on the drawings, photos pinned where you took them, weather attached automatically. You leave the site, the PDF is done.
>
> And because it's not a report-only tool, the observation that becomes an RFI doesn't get re-typed — it's already in the same system as your RFIs, submittals, and punch lists.
>
> Join the waitlist → buildcycle.io/field-reports

## Post 5 — CA war story (the pain that made BC exist)

> BuildCycle started with a pay application and a bad afternoon.
>
> I'm an architect. I was reviewing a contractor's pay app in a PDF, cross-checking it against a spreadsheet I kept on the side, against a change order log I kept somewhere else. The numbers didn't tie. They never quite tied. Every month was a small forensic exercise to answer one question: what is this contract actually worth right now?
>
> The software built for the GC wasn't going to solve that — it answers to them. The tools built for my side stopped at the field report.
>
> So the contract math lived in my head and a spreadsheet, which is a terrible place for the number everyone's decisions depend on.
>
> BuildCycle keeps that number live. Every change order, every pay app, reflected the moment it's entered. No month-end archaeology.
>
> That's the whole origin: I wanted the current number to just… be current.
>
> Join the waitlist → buildcycle.io

## Post 6 — "What CA software actually costs in 2026" (repurposes the comparison pages)

> What does construction administration software actually cost a design firm in 2026? I priced it out.
>
> Per-seat platforms: $150–200 per user, per month. A five-person team clears $9,000/year before anyone's touched a pay app — and half of them are rationed off the license anyway.
>
> Report-only field tools: cheaper per head, but they stop at the site visit. You're still running submittals, change orders, and pay apps somewhere else. You didn't buy one system; you bought part of one.
>
> Per-project flat pricing: you pay for the work in front of you, not the size of your org chart. Unlimited users. A solo architect with two projects and a fifty-person firm both pay for exactly what they're administering.
>
> Guess which one we built. Full breakdown on the pricing page — buildcycle.io/pricing. Join the waitlist while you're there.
